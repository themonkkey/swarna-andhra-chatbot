About

What This Assistant Is

The Swarna Andhra GVA Assistant is a question and answer tool built by Pahlé India Foundation for the state capacity building programme. It helps district, constituency, and mandal level officers find clear answers about how state and district income is measured, and how they can raise it.

The programme trains officers on GDP, GSDP, and GDDP estimation methodology, and suggests ways to improve Gross Value Added across sectors through specific, practical interventions drawn from real case studies.

Who it is for

  • District and constituency officers who need quick, reliable answers grounded in official material.
  • Master trainers preparing sessions on estimation methodology.
  • Planning teams looking at where a district has comparative advantage and what to do about it.

How it works

When you ask a question, the assistant searches the full training corpus, the official DDP and GSVA source documents, and the district data workbook. It then answers using that material and always shows which document and page it drew from, so every answer can be traced back to its source.

The assistant covers four areas: estimation methodology, GVA calculation, district economic profiles with suggested interventions, and district GSDP and DDP figures.

Where the answers come from

  • The official Guideline for Compilation of DDP Estimates from the National Statistics Office.
  • The Compilation of GSVA Estimates with base year 2022-23.
  • The DDP 2024-25 FRE book with district figures.
  • Training decks, toolkits, and fourteen sector case studies prepared for the programme.
Methodology

How District Income Is Estimated

This summary follows the official Guideline for Compilation of District Domestic Product Estimates issued by the National Statistics Office, consistent with the revised GSVA series under base year 2022-23.

District Domestic Product, or DDP, measures the total value of goods and services produced within a district over a year. It is the district level counterpart of GSDP at the state level and GDP at the national level. It helps compare districts, identify regions that need attention, and plan local development.

For estimation, the economy of a district is divided into eleven broad industries: agriculture and allied, mining, manufacturing, electricity and utilities, construction, trade and hotels, transport and communication, financial services, real estate and professional services, public administration and defence, and other services.

The three approaches

Each of the eleven industries is estimated using whichever of these three approaches best fits the data available for it.

Top-down

State to district

State level totals are estimated first, then shared among districts using suitable indicators. Used when direct district data is not available. It keeps district figures consistent with state totals, but may miss local variation.

Bottom-up

District first

Values are estimated directly from data collected within each district, then added up to reach the state total. It reflects local conditions best, but needs detailed and reliable district data.

Mixed

Best of both

Bottom-up is used for sectors where reliable district data exists, and top-down for the rest. This balances accuracy against what data is actually available.

From GVA to district income

Gross Value Added for each sector is adjusted and added up to give the district total, then taxes and subsidies bring it to the product measure.

Gross District Value Added (GDVA) = sum of adjusted GVA across all sectors
GDDP = GDVA + Taxes on Products − Subsidies on Products
Net figures: NVA = GVA − Consumption of Fixed Capital, then NDDP = NDVA + Taxes − Subsidies

Per capita district income is the total district income divided by the mid-year projected population of the district. It is the common measure of the standard of living in a district.

Which sector uses which method

Crop and livestock estimates lean on production and yield data at the district level, so they use bottom-up methods. Large scale manufacturing and organised services often rely on state totals shared out by indicators, so they use top-down methods. Most districts end up using the mixed approach across their eleven industries.
