West Godavari, East Godavari and Krishna are among the most important rice-producing districts of Andhra Pradesh and India.

Rice monoculture often faces cost, price and climate risks. Fish adds a second output from the same field and water.

BASIC CONCEPT

The model: one paddy field, two harvests

How the symbiosis works

Fish help rice

Fish manure fertilizes soil; fish movement aerates water and softens soil; fish eat larvae, weeds and insects.

Rice helps fish

Rice canopy provides shade; the flooded field gives habitat; plankton and organic matter support fish growth.

Field modification

Raised bunds, fish refuge trench/pond, screened inlet/outlet, controlled water depth and safe harvest channel.

Management shift

Rice calendar must align with stocking; pesticide use must be avoided or carefully controlled.

Core lesson: this is not "fish added to paddy"; it is a planned field system requiring water control, species choice and farmer training.

Sources: FAO GIAHS Qingtian Rice-Fish Culture; UNDP/NITI Assam rice-fish case.

Why this model is important

Widely practiced in the Apatani plateau and described by the state fisheries department as profitable and sustainable.

Generates two crops — paddy and fish — from the same cultivable area.

Uses low-lying fields, raised bunds, irrigation channels and suitable carp species.

GLOBAL HERITAGE MODEL

China case study: Qingtian Rice–Fish Culture System

1,300+ yrs

traditional system in Qingtian

Eco-brand

What makes it successful

Fish fertilize rice, disturb water, soften soil, and eat larvae and weeds.

Rice gives fish shade, food and habitat.

Lower-cost inputs reduce use of chemical fertilizers, pesticides and herbicides.

Heritage branding supports higher-value products and rural revitalization.

Learning for AP: protect ecology + build product identity. This can become a "safe rice + local fish" brand, not just a production technique.

SUCCESS FACTORS

What the India and China cases have in common

Right site

Low-lying, water-retentive fields with controlled inlet/outlet.

Field design

Strong bunds, trenches/refuge pond, screened flow points and harvest channel.

Species choice

Carp or locally suited species matched to water depth, salinity and crop calendar.

Input discipline

Avoid harmful pesticides; shift to natural/safer pest management.

Technical handholding

Regular visits by agriculture + fisheries teams during first crop cycle.

Market linkage

Farmer groups, aggregators, fish markets and cold chain for better returns.

Officer takeaway: pilots should be selected for technical feasibility first, then scaled through farmer confidence and demonstrated profitability.

Sources synthesized from Assam case, Arunachal Pradesh fisheries note and FAO Qingtian GIAHS documentation.

WHERE TO APPLY

Relevance for paddy-growing districts of Andhra Pradesh

Prioritise districts/mandals with:

Contiguous paddy area and assured water retention.

Existing canal or tank-based irrigation with manageable drainage.

Farmer interest in natural farming / lower chemical input practices.

Access to fish seed, local markets and fisheries extension.

Low-lying paddy patches where waterlogging is common.

Potential AP focus areas

Delta paddy belts

Krishna, East/West Godavari and adjoining delta mandals can test field-refuge models.

High paddy districts

ANGRAU paddy outlook identifies West Godavari, Krishna, Srikakulam and Kakinada as major paddy-growing districts.

APCNF alignment

Integrated model can reduce chemical dependence and support biodiversity if field protocols are followed.

Nutrition & income

Freshwater fish availability improves household protein while rice yield is protected or improved.

Do not universalise. Start with feasibility screening and demonstration mandals before district-wide scale-up.

Sources: AP Seeds; ANGRAU Paddy Outlook 2023–24; CGIAR 2023 note on IRFS in West Godavari.

2. Demonstration clusters

Start with 5–10 fields per mandal through volunteer farmers, FPOs or natural farming groups.

Pool agriculture, fisheries, RBK, MGNREGS, PMMSY/NFSM and local training resources.

Tie up local fish vendors, ice boxes, collection points and rice branding before harvest.

Adoption

Farmers trained, pilots completed, replication demand

Market

Fish price, buyer linkage, aggregation volume

Three takeaways for AP officials

1. Start small, but design properly

Pilot only in technically suitable fields with water control and farmer consent.

2. Converge agriculture and fisheries teams

Joint field visits are essential during design, stocking and harvest.

3. Show income evidence

Compare with paddy-only plots; scale only when pilots show net returns and risk control.

Final message: rice–fish farming is a district productivity strategy, not just a farm technique.
