Key lessons for district and mandal officials

What made the model work—and what should be replicated carefully.

1

Local government must anchor the platform

Mandal/district administration gives legitimacy and continuity.

2

Private sector concerns need practical solutions

Price, quality and reliability committees make procurement possible.

3

Community confidence has to be built

Use regular hotel-community interactions, exposure visits and transparent processes.

4

Capacity building must be demand-led

Train people in skills linked to actual hotel/visitor demand.

5

Destination assets must be protected

Tourism income depends on wetlands, beaches, culture, cleanliness and trust.

Training prompt: Which 2–3 assets in your mandal can be converted into community-led tourism products without overuse?

Replication strategy for Andhra Pradesh

A practical sequence district and mandal teams can adapt to local tourism assets.

1

Identify tourism anchors

Map beaches, temples, crafts, food, wetlands, hills, tribal circuits and agri-tourism points.

2

Create a destination task force

Include Mandal Parishad, Gram Panchayat, Tourism, DRDA/SHGs, hotels, transporters, youth and artisans.

3

Map local supply and skills

List farmers, fishers, SHGs, artisans, cooks, guides and available facilities.

4

Match supply with demand

Run structured discussions with hotels/tour operators to define price, quality, quantity and delivery.

5

Pilot and monitor

Launch 1–2 low-risk experiences and procurement linkages; track income, participation, cleanliness and visitor feedback.

Replicate the principles, not the exact product: coastal mandals may focus on fishing/boat experiences; temple towns on local food, crafts and heritage walks; tribal/hill mandals on guided nature and culture routes.

Source: NITI for States Best Practice ID 101989; UNWTO Tourism for Development, Vol. II, Case Study 9.

90-day action plan and monitoring metrics

Use the case study to move from awareness to a small, visible pilot.

0–15 days

Asset & stakeholder mapping

Tourism assets, hotels, SHGs, producers, existing schemes

15–30 days

Task force + MoU structure

Roles, meeting rhythm, price/quality norms, grievance loop

30–60 days

Capacity building + supply groups

Guide training, food/craft hygiene, production calendars

60–90 days

Pilot product launch

Village/heritage/craft/eco experience + procurement linkage

Suggested monitoring metrics

Local income generated

No. of families / SHGs linked

No. of tourists using local experiences

Hotel procurement from local groups

Waste management and cleanliness status

Visitor satisfaction / complaints

Closing activity: Each official identifies one pilot destination, one community group and one private-sector partner to begin with.
