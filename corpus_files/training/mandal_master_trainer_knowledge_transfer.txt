CONTEXT

Why a master trainer model is needed

Learning becomes useful only when it is transferred, practiced and tracked at the field level.

Bridge training to action

Master trainers connect state-level vision and technical concepts with the day-to-day work of mandal officers.

Build common understanding

They ensure uniform interpretation of GDP/GVA, KPIs and sectoral strategies across mandals.

Enable replication

They help officers apply tools, indicators and local exercises in their own mandals.

Creates a cadre of officers who can explain the modules in simple language.

Improves field ownership of Swarna Andhra priorities.

Reduces dependence on one-time classroom training.

MASTER TRAINER MANDATE

Role of mandal officer as master trainer

A master trainer converts technical training into a structured learning experience for peers and field teams.

Interpreter

Translate policy vision and economic concepts into mandal-level language.

Facilitator

Run discussions, exercises and group work rather than only lecturing.

Mentor

Support officers in identifying local indicators and growth interventions.

Quality anchor

Ensure consistency of concepts, tools and reporting formats.

Feedback channel

Capture field-level doubts, data gaps and implementation challenges.

MODULE 2 DELIVERY

How to teach GDP / GSDP / GDDP / GMDP / GVA simply

Use a simple ladder: production at different geographic levels, and value addition across sectors.

GDP

Value of goods and services produced in the country

GSDP

State-level equivalent of GDP

GDDP

District-level estimate of economic output

GMDP

Mandal-level estimate used for local planning

GVA

Value added by primary, secondary and tertiary sectors

Trainer tip

Use one relatable example: paddy → rice mill → transport/retail. Show how each step adds value and how sectors are linked.

MODULE 2 DELIVERY

Explain top-down and bottom-up approaches

Participants should understand both approaches because mandal planning requires both official estimates and field-based validation.

Top-down approach

Start with higher-level data and distribute it to districts / mandals using suitable indicators such as area, production, workforce or enterprise counts.

Bottom-up approach

Start from local data, field records and sectoral indicators, then aggregate to estimate mandal or district contribution.

Use together for stronger estimates

GMDP PRACTICE

Run hands-on exercises, not only lectures

The technical module includes hands-on examples. The master trainer should reproduce this through small, guided exercises.

1

Choose one local subsector

Example: paddy, dairy, handloom, fisheries, retail, tourism.

2

Identify local data points

Production, area, enterprises, workforce, sales, service users or scheme coverage.

3

Discuss value addition

Where is value created, where is it lost, and what can be improved?

4

Convert into action

Data improvement, convergence, scheme targeting or market facilitation.

Exercise output

Each group should submit one mandal sector worksheet: key data, key bottleneck, one intervention and one KPI.

MODULE 3 DELIVERY

Connect KPIs to sectoral GVA

KPIs should help officers see whether value addition is likely improving in primary, secondary and tertiary sectors.

Primary sector

Yield, cropping intensity, livestock productivity, fish production, marketable surplus, irrigation coverage.

Secondary sector

Number of active enterprises, production value, power use, formalization, credit uptake, jobs created.

Tertiary sector

Tourist footfall, retail activity, transport movement, digital transactions, service delivery volumes.

Trainer question: "Which 5 indicators are already available in your mandal records, and which 5 need better data collection?"

MODULE 4 DELIVERY

Teach strategies for boosting primary sector GVA

Use the DDP toolkit approach: identify productivity, value chain and market bottlenecks, then select interventions that raise value addition.

Productivity

Improve yield through better seed, soil health, irrigation, extension, animal health and technology adoption.

Post-harvest value

Reduce losses through storage, grading, aggregation, processing and better logistics.

Market access

Link farmers / producers with FPOs, e-NAM, procurement, input-output markets and institutional buyers.

Ask officers to choose one primary subsector and identify the weakest link in the local value chain.

Convert the selected bottleneck into one convergence action and one KPI.

MODULE 4 DELIVERY

Teach strategies for boosting secondary sector GVA

Secondary sector growth depends on enterprise productivity, investment facilitation, skilled manpower and stronger local supply chains.

Enterprise mapping

Identify active, dormant and potential units; map clusters, constraints and market linkages.

Ease of doing business

Support clearances, infrastructure issues, land / power / logistics coordination and scheme awareness.

Skilling & jobs

Link ITIs, apprenticeships, industry demand and local employment opportunities.

Map units

Diagnose constraints

Converge departments

Track output & jobs

Training prompt

"Which local enterprise activity can be scaled in your mandal, and what data will prove that value addition is increasing?"

MODULE 4 DELIVERY

Teach strategies for boosting tertiary sector GVA

Services grow when local demand, connectivity, digital systems and visitor/consumer experience improve.

Tourism & local services

Improve site management, signage, sanitation, homestays, guides and local enterprise participation.

Trade & logistics

Strengthen mandal markets, storage, transport access, aggregation and digital payments.

Human development services

Track service delivery volumes in health, education, skilling and citizen services to improve efficiency.

Use a service chain lens: demand → access → quality → spending → jobs.

Ask officers to identify one underperforming service activity that can be improved through convergence.

HOW TO TEACH EFFECTIVELY

Facilitation methods for adult officers

Mandal-level officers bring practical experience. Use methods that draw out their knowledge and connect it to the modules.

Start with experience

Ask: "Which sector matters most in your mandal and why?"

Use simple examples

Explain GVA through one local product or service chain.

Mix lecture with activity

After every 20–25 minutes, add a question, poll or group task.

Make outputs visible

Use charts, worksheets and action notes so learning is captured.

Link to daily work

End each concept with how officers can use it in planning or review.

TRAINER CONFIDENCE

Handle questions, doubts and misconceptions

Master trainers do not need to know every answer immediately. They need a structured way to clarify, park and follow up.

Listen

Clarify

Connect to module

Park if needed

Follow up

Common misconception

"GVA is only a statistical concept."

Correction: GVA helps officers identify which activities create value and where interventions can improve productivity.

Common misconception

"KPIs are only for reporting."

Correction: KPIs should trigger decisions, such as targeted support, convergence or corrective action.

Maintain a "parking lot" of questions requiring data or higher-level clarification.

Document recurring doubts and share them with district/state training anchors.

FROM LEARNING TO IMPLEMENTATION

Convert every KT session into an action plan

The session should not end with only attendance. It should end with a specific mandal-level action note.

Sector / subsector

Which activity will be targeted?

Current issue

What bottleneck or data gap is affecting value addition?

Intervention

What can be done through convergence or scheme facilitation?

KPI

How will improvement be tracked?

Timeline & owner

Who will do what by when?

Output standard

Every participant leaves with at least one action note that is realistic, measurable and linked to sectoral GVA.
