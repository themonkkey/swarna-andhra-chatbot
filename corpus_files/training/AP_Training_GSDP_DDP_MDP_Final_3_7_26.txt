Introduction

Core Concepts

What is National Income and why do we measure it?

Planning: They are also used to formulate investment and tax policies, as well as budgetary allocations, to remove obstacles to economic growth.

Monitoring: NIAs are used to track the progress of plans to raise productivity, output, and welfare.

Comparative analysis: They can be used to compare the performance of countries or even regions within a country.

National income is the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced by a country's residents over a specific period, usually one year.

National income accounts (NIAs) are fundamental aggregate statistics used in planning and monitoring. It provides a measure of citizens' well-being through per capita income and its growth over time. It can also be used to assess the level and growth rate of productivity.

Importance of National Income

Core Concepts

Effective planning needs a quick and accurate estimation of national income.

To facilitate comparison, estimation should follow a set of unified principles; national income can be estimated in various ways.

Most widely used measure of national income: Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

At the state level, we estimate State Gross Domestic Product (GSDP)

In India, NIAs were normally undertaken up to the state level,

Andhra Pradesh also compiles Gross Domestic Product at the district level, constituency level, and mandal level

Estimates made by the Directorate of Economics & Statistics, which follows the MOSPI guidelines (2026)

Introduction to the Tertiary Sector

The tertiary sector covers market and non-market services that support households, enterprises and government operations.

Market services

Trade, hotels, transport, communication, financial services, real estate, professional services and information services.

Government services

Public administration, defence and public delivery of education, health and other services.

Unincorporated enterprises, own-account workers, domestic services and ownership of dwellings.

District Level Estimation

Conceptual foundation for economic measurement and district-level planning

Why District Domestic Product?

Accurate and reliable DDP estimates support effective policy-making

Balanced regional growth:

Compare economic performance between districts

Identify backward regions

Planning local development programs

In recent years, DDP has gained importance as a key component in assessing regional human development

One of the three core indicators for computing the Human Development Index (HDI).

Why district estimates are harder than state estimates

More local detail usually means more data constraints

1

Data availability

Some sector data exists only at state level or with weak district identifiers.

2

Informality

A large share of activity may be unregistered or not reported in routine systems.

3

Headquarter effects

Services or company records may be booked in one location but delivered elsewhere.

4

Proxy dependence

District estimates often require allocation keys or proxy indicators.

5

Comparability risk

Districts differ in area, population, sector mix and administrative capacity.

Therefore, district estimates should always be read with their methodology, data source and validation trail.

GDDP Estimation Methodology

District Domestic Product estimates support decentralised planning by comparing inter-district economic structure, performance and sectoral disparities.

State sector estimate

Official GVA / GSDP estimate at state level

District data / allocator

Direct district production data where available; proxy or physical indicator where direct data is weak

District sector GVA

Sector-wise value allocated or estimated for each district

GDDP / PCI

Aggregate district economy and per capita income for planning

Core rule

Use the same sectoral logic as state accounts wherever district-level data are available. Where data are scarce, apportion state estimates using the best available district indicator.

Key caution

For many non-primary sectors, district estimates are broad indicators because direct district data remain limited and estimates are revised when firmer data arrive.

State sector GVA

District allocation key

District-wise sector GVA

A mixed approach is used.

Primary sector methodology I: Agriculture, horticulture and livestock

Agriculture + Horticulture

• Major crops: district-wise value of output = district production × district price.

• If district prices are unavailable, use regional / neighbouring district prices where available.

• If no regional price is available, use state-level prices.

• Miscellaneous/unspecified crops: if only district area is available, value of output = district area × state-level VPH of similar crops.

• Inputs are allocated across districts in proportion to district GVO before deriving district GVA.

Livestock

• Milk, meat, wool, cocoons and honey: district production × average wholesale prices.

• Meat by-products: state GVO allocated by district-wise meat production.

• Dung: state GVO allocated by district-wise bovine, sheep and goat population.

• Eggs: state GVO allocated by district-wise egg production.

• Poultry meat: state GVO allocated by district-wise poultry population.

• Increment in livestock: state GVO allocated by district-wise livestock population.

Primary sector methodology II: Forestry, fishing and mining

Forestry & Logging

• Major and minor forest produce: state GVA apportioned by district-wise forest area.

• Trees Outside Forest (TOF): state GVA apportioned by area under pulp and timber.

• Fuel wood: state GVA apportioned by rural and urban population.

• Fodder: state GVA apportioned by livestock projected population based on 2019 Census.

Fishing

• District-wise value of inland fish, marine fish and prawns is estimated by multiplying district-wise production with corresponding prices.

• This is closer to direct estimation than proxy apportionment when district production and prices are available.

Mining & Quarrying

• State GVA is allocated to districts on the basis of district-wise production of major and minor minerals.

• District production data should be reconciled with mining records and mineral-wise reporting.

4

Secondary sector methodology: Manufacturing, utilities and construction

Manufacturing

Organised

• Public + private corporate GVA allocated using district-wise ASI GVA provided by NSO, Kolkata.

• NDEs allocated on the basis of location.

Unorganised/unincorporated

• State GVA allocated by district-wise ASI non-corporate GVA provided by ASI, NSO, Kolkata (Code 1 & 2).

Electricity, gas, water supply and remediation

Electricity

• Generation: plant location and generated capacity.

• Transmission/distribution: district-wise power consumption.

Gas

• NDE gas: district-wise natural gas production.

• Bio-gas: number of bio-gas plants.

Water supply / Remediation

• District GVA from water supply and sanitary services in local body accounts.

Construction

Household sector

• RRBs: rural households, Census 2011.

• URBs: urban households, Census 2011.

• NRB & OCW: urban households, Census 2011.

• Plantation: area of plantation in agriculture.

• Other households: urban households, Census 2011.

Public/residual

• State/local body GVA; railways by railway line length; residual by urban households.

Tertiary sector methodology I: Trade, hotels, restaurants, transport and communication

Trade, hotels and restaurants

Trade

• Public: district-wise paddy procured from Civil Supplies Department.

• Private corporate + unincorporated: district-wise total tax revenue collections.

Hotels & restaurants

• Public, private corporate and unincorporated components: district-wise number of tourists, domestic and foreign.

Railways, transport and storage

Railways

• State GVA apportioned using district-wise number of employees.

Road transport

• Public: APSRTC employees.

• Private corporate + unincorporated: registered vehicles.

Water / air transport

• Cargo handled at ports; passenger air traffic, location-based.

Incidental services / storage

• NDE location, combined transport growth, warehousing capacity and cold storage capacity.

Communication and broadcasting

• District estimates for communication are compiled by apportioning state GVA using district-wise urban households as per Census 2011.

Why this matters

• Urban households serve as a structural proxy for communication demand and service usage.

Tertiary sector methodology II: Finance, real estate, public administration and other services

Banking & Insurance

• State GVA apportioned to districts using district-wise bank credits and deposits.

Real estate & ownership of dwellings

Real estate

• Public: number of non-agricultural enterprises from 6th Economic Census.

• Real estate + renting: real estate workforce.

• Computer and R&D: professional, scientific and technical activities from 6th Economic Census.

• Unincorporated rent + admin: real estate workforce.

Ownership of dwellings

• Urban: urban households, Census 2011.

• Rural: rural households, Census 2011.

Public administration

Central and State Government

• District-wise number of employees working in all departments and local bodies.

• Source: Director, Office of Treasuries & Accounts.

Autonomous institutions

• District-wise GVA of autonomous institution accounts compiled by DE&S.

Other services

Education / Health

• Public: employees, local body GVA, autonomous institution accounts.

• Private corporate + unincorporated: employees in private sector.

Remaining other services

• District-wise urban population, Census 2011.

Illustrative allocation example

| Mandal | Indicator value: Paddy production (tonnes) | Share | District agriculture-paddy GVA (₹ lakh) | Allocated mandal GVA (₹ lakh) |
| :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
| A | 10,000 | 10,000 / 40,000 = 25.0% | 4,000 | 1,000 |
| B | 15,000 | 15,000 / 40,000 = 37.5% | 4,000 | 1,500 |
| C | 5,000 | 5,000 / 40,000 = 12.5% | 4,000 | 500 |
| D | 10,000 | 10,000 / 40,000 = 25.0% | 4,000 | 1,000 |
| District total | 40,000 | 100.0% | 4,000 | 4,000 |

Interpretation

The mandal with a higher share of paddy production receives a higher share of the district paddy GVA. This does not imply higher productivity unless yield, prices, and input costs are separately examined.

Wrap-up: the five takeaways

1

GVA measures value added

Not just production, sales or expenditure.

2

GSDP/GDDP need methodology

Always read estimates with source, method and revision status.

3

Validation is central

Triangulate official estimates, administrative data and proxies.

4

Action closes the loop

Measurement becomes management only through assigned follow-up.

5. Final message: data should not end at reporting; it should improve diagnosis, prioritization, and administrative decisions.
