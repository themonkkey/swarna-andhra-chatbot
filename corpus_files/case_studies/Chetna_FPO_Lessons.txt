Why This Case Study Matters to You

Small and marginal farmers face the same pressures across India — and the same solutions apply to your mandal.

Poor returns

Input of Rs. 2000 returned only Rs. 1500 — farming became unviable and farmers quit cotton.

Exploitation at the mandi

Middlemen cheated farmers on weight, quality and price; mandi rates often fell below MSP.

Degraded land

Heavy chemical use ruined soil health, raising costs season after season.

No collective strength

Acting alone, a small farmer had no bargaining power and no access to credit or markets.

The Three-Tier Farmer Structure

Every farmer is simultaneously a member at all levels — ownership and decisions stay with farmers.

Village level

Self-Help Group

10–20 farmers. Meet monthly on inputs, finance, standards & marketing. Monitor that organic norms are followed.

Block / District level

Cooperative

600–700 farmers. A legal entity that can take loans, aggregate produce and hold sub-licence for certification.

National level

Producer Company (COAPCL)

Farmer-owned trading arm. Sells directly to retailers in India and abroad; holds organic & fair-trade certification.

Democratic, bottom-up: board members of the producer company are elected from each cooperative.

Four Things That Made It Work

The pillars behind Chetna's success — the elements worth replicating.

1. Trust through visible results

First worked with a few farmers for 2–3 years to prove organic farming lowers input cost and stabilises yield before scaling up.

2. A systemised supply chain

Eliminated middlemen; cooperatives lobbied for their own market place and the producer company linked farmers to buyers.

3. A competitive niche

Organic + fair trade created exclusivity. Organic farming saved \~Rs. 4,000 per acre by cutting chemical inputs.

4. Long-term buyer relationships

Direct ties with retailers, quality assurance, sample sharing, and barcode traceability of every batch.

Lessons for Strengthening Your FPO

What a mandal officer can act on — Part 1 of 2

Start small, build genuine ownership

Form tight SHGs of 10–20 farmers that meet monthly. Federate them upward. Let farmers make the decisions, so the FPO truly belongs to them.

Demonstrate before you scale

Run a small demo plot for 2–3 seasons. Visible results convert sceptical farmers far better than a meeting ever will.

Aggregate to gain bargaining power

Pool produce through the FPO so members negotiate as one. Use the APMC reforms to create your own collection point and skip exploitative middlemen.

Lessons for Strengthening Your FPO

What a mandal officer can act on — Part 2 of 2

Build a niche & certification

Help the FPO move into organic, GI or fair-trade lines where margins are higher. Certification and traceability (e.g. barcoding) command premium prices.

Forge direct, lasting buyer links

Connect the FPO to institutional and export buyers. Direct relationships and assured quality replace one-off, low-price mandi sales.

Make it self-sustaining & transparent

Use trade profits to fund certification and operations. Build internal control systems and bottom-up monitoring so the FPO outlives any subsidy or officer.

Your Role as a Mandal Officer

A practical checklist to take back to the field.

Identify & mobilise

Map willing small/marginal farmers; help them form and register SHGs and cooperatives.

Enable, don't control

Facilitate meetings and capacity-building; keep ownership and voting with farmers.

Link to schemes & credit

Connect the FPO to institutional credit, insurance, NREGS works and govt. schemes.

Open markets

Use APMC reforms; help set up collection centres and connect to bulk/export buyers.

Add value

Push for organic / certified / processed lines that earn a premium over raw produce.

Ensure transparency

Set up simple internal monitoring so members trust the system and it stays sustainable.

THE ONE TAKEAWAY

When farmers own the supply chain, they capture the value.

A strong FPO is not built by an officer issuing orders — it is built by helping farmers organise, prove the model on the ground, and connect directly to markets. That is how Chetna turned abandoned cotton fields into a premium, farmer-owned business.
