THE GLOBAL & POLICY CONTEXT

India is the world's second-largest producer of textiles & garments and its sixth-largest exporter, holding about 4% of global textile & apparel trade.

The global apparel market is worth \~$1.9 trillion (2025); world textile & apparel trade is projected to reach $1.2 trillion by 2030.

New FTAs — the India-UK FTA, UAE CEPA and Australia ECTA — plus the “China+1” sourcing shift are reshaping market access.

India targets $100 bn (Rs 9 lakh Cr) of textile exports by 2030, up from \~$37 bn — a leap that frames Tiruppur's strategic importance.

India's textile & apparel exports touched $36.6 bn (Rs 3.3 lakh Cr) in FY25; readymade garments led at $16 bn, 49% of it knitwear.

DEFINING FEATURES OF THE CLUSTER

Cotton-based knitted garments — T-shirts, polos, sportswear, nightwear and children's wear.

Mostly proprietorship / partnership firms under family management.

Many units specialise in cutting, making and trimming (CMT) of knitted fabric.

Few vertically integrated plants — a high degree of subcontracting across knitting, processing and finishing.

15+

active industry associations settle disputes, ease formalities and lobby government.

Household roots

Built on self-funded household workshops; specialised skills evolved into a globally competitive cluster.

What Drives — and Constrains — the Cluster

06

ENGINES OF GROWTH

Proximity to Coimbatore

Close to a major cotton-spinning hub — fast, reliable access to yarn.

Speed to market

Customised samples in under 12 hours; half a million pieces in days.

Entrepreneurial management

Personalised, family-run control of negotiations and operations.

Pro-active marketing

Buyers from 35+ countries; ZLD-led “Green Tiruppur” brand wins ESG-minded orders.

STRUCTURAL CONSTRAINTS

Tariff disadvantage against FTA-backed rivals such as Bangladesh and Vietnam.

Mainly commodity / price competition with limited high-income market knowledge.

Cotton-heavy product mix while global demand shifts to man-made fibre (MMF).

Rising labour and raw-material costs — polyester \~33% costlier than in China.

Infrastructure growth lags far behind the pace of industrial expansion.

POLICY INCENTIVES

PM MITRA — seven mega textile parks with world-class, plug-and-play infrastructure to anchor scale.

PLI Scheme for Textiles — incentives for MMF apparel, MMF fabrics and technical textiles.

RoSCTL & RoDTEP — rebates of embedded central and state taxes on garment exports.

SAMARTH skilling, plus Tamil Nadu's Rs 1,250 Cr plan for women workers' hostels over five years.

Private-sector drive remains Tiruppur's engine — now amplified by schemes targeting $100 bn of textile exports by 2030.

THE STRAIN

Dyeing & bleaching effluents (caustic soda, acids, peroxides) once choked the Noyyal & Nallar rivers.

Groundwater was depleted and contaminated — unfit for domestic, industrial or farm use.

In 2011 the Madras High Court ordered all \~700 dyeing & bleaching units closed until zero discharge was achieved.

THE RESPONSE

By 2012, 18 CETPs and individual ETPs reopened with full Zero Liquid Discharge — a world first for a textile cluster.

RO, evaporation and salt-recovery systems now reclaim both water and salts, cutting input costs.

Wind & solar power and mass tree planting underpin the carbon-conscious “Green Tiruppur” strategy.

OVERALL ASSESSMENT

SWOT — Tiruppur's Textile & Garments Cluster

STRENGTHS

Strong raw-material base & cheap, flexible labour

Hard-working, innovative entrepreneurs

Strong industry associations & inter-firm trust

World-first ZLD & green-energy credentials win ESG buyers

WEAKNESSES

Under-used machinery; low-end, seasonal focus

High input costs — polyester & viscose pricier than rivals'

Low labour productivity; rising wage costs

Reliance on foreign design; little vertical integration

OPPORTUNITIES

India-UK FTA & UAE CEPA open duty-free markets

MMF, technical textiles & value-added fashion-wear

“China+1” & Bangladesh disruption shifting orders to India

PM MITRA parks; Rs 1 lakh Cr export target by 2030

THREATS

Duty-free rivals — Bangladesh & Vietnam at full capacity

US tariffs & EU sustainability rules (CSDDD, traceability)

Cotton price volatility & costly man-made fibre inputs

Rival textile hubs rising in nearby districts
